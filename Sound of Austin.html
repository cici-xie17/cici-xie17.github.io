Problem
This project was part of The Accessibility Internet Rally (AIR) which pairs designers and developers to build accessible websites for non-profits. Our non-profit was Sound of Austin, a public art project seeking to help the city of Austin find its most unique sound every day. The client had started with the beginnings of a website but it was confusing, visually unappealing, and unfinished. It was not clear what the website was meant to do and what the project was.
Solution
Through stakeholder interviews, we figured out that the goal of the website was to educate visitors on what the project was, and recruit musicians, vendors, and developers to participate in creating a music aggregation platform. We created a website that allows people to quickly understand what the project is and sign up to particpate with ease.

My Contribution
I conducted stakeholder Interviews during the research phase to ensure I knew what the client wanted since the website concept was very unique. I also took a rudimentary sketch the client had for a logo and created multiple iterations, resulting in a new modern logo.

I was the sole designer on the team and created all wireframes from low to high fidelity with strong emphasis on accessibility, usability, and WCAG compliance. In addition, I also worked closely with developers for a cleaner developer hand off process.

RESEARCH
Stakeholder Interviews
I started by interviewing the client to identify their key problems and delineating user needs. This part of the research phase was arduous because the client had difficulty defining their scope. It required multiple interviews to figure out that they wanted our team to create a website that raised awareness of the art project and recruited volunteers to help build a music aggregation website, and that we were not creating the music aggregation website itself. We were finally able to refine the scope to reflect these three primary aspects:
I also learned what the client preferred in terms of visual design. They wanted me to incorporate the old school blue audio equalizer, the color gradient from the Instagram logo, and photos of crowds/concerts/Austin city skyline.
Competitive Analysis
I conducted a competitive analysis to try and better understand the landscape. Because the concept for this website was so specific, it wasn’t really possible to find other products in this problem space. Instead, I turned to presidential campaign websites because they shared the most similar primary initiatives for users.
I found that:
  • Most campaign websites have a large, loudly colored "Donate" button in the top right hand corner
  • The landing page always has a large hero image or an engaging visual element
  • Somewhere on the homepage, typically near the top is a form that allows you to quickly sign up and only ask users to input 2-3 pieces of information

    RETROSPECTIVE
    Biggest Challenges
    Trying to understand and define the scope of the website was very difficult given the unique product. We continued to run into scope creep as the client tried to have us develop the AI music aggregator itself, instead of a website that informs users and recruits volunteers for the project.
    What I Learned
    Because this website redesign was part of the Internet Accessibility Rally, I had to go through web accessibility training and learned a lot about using sufficient contrast, alt text, and not using color alone to convey information.

    I also learned how to stand my ground as a designer with clients asking for more and more features that were not discussed in the original user flow. By defending the core user needs and not adding on unneccessary features I was able to make a cleaner and more streamlined website.

    This was my second time working on a full product team with a PM and developers, and I learned to involve the engineers often and early in order to make the design hand off as smooth as possible.
    What I Could've Improved
    SGiven the opportunity, I would’ve done user interviews early in the research phase given that this product is so unique and we only had client interview data. I also would’ve engaged in usability testing after the first round of Hi-Fi wireframes in order to get some actual user feedback on the website.
